History of Jablapur

"It is believed that Jabalpur is the tapasya bhumi of a mythological figure known as sage Jabali, from whom the name Jabalpur is derived. Ashokan relics have been found. Later on it was the capital of the famous Tripuri kingdom in the 9th & 10th centuries. In AD 875, it was taken over by the Kalachuri dynasty who made Jabalpur their capital. In the 13th century, the Gonds seized it and made it their capital. Inscriptions record the existence during the 11th and 12th centuries of a local line of princes of the Haihai people who are closely connected with the history of Gondwana.In the 16th century the Gond raja of Garha-Mandla extended his power over fifty-two districts, including the present Jabalpur. During the minority of his grandson, Asaf Khan, the viceroy of Kara Manikpur, conquered the Garha principality and held it at first as an independent chief. Eventually he submitted to the Mughal emperor Akbar. From time to time, the Mughal rulers tried to overrun it. The legendary Gond Queen Rani Durgavati also died fighting the Mughal forces led by the great Mughal Emperor Akbar."

History and Administration

Jabalpur is a city located in the central Indian state of Madhya Pradesh. It has a rich history and has been an important center of administration for centuries. The history of Jabalpur dates back to ancient times. The city was known as Tripuri in ancient times and was the capital of the Chedi dynasty. The city was later ruled by various dynasties, including the Mauryas, Guptas, and Mughals. In the 18th century, Jabalpur came under the control of the Marathas, who made it an important center of administration. In the 19th century, Jabalpur became a part of the British Empire. The British established a cantonment in the city and made it an important center of administration for the Central Provinces. During this time, Jabalpur saw significant development in infrastructure, education, and industry. After India gained independence in 1947, Jabalpur became a part of the newly formed state of Madhya Pradesh. The city continued to be an important center of administration and saw significant development in various sectors, including education, healthcare, and industry. Today, Jabalpur is the third-largest city in Madhya Pradesh and is an important center of administration, education, and industry. The city is governed by the Jabalpur Municipal Corporation, which is responsible for providing various civic services to the residents of the city. The corporation is headed by a mayor and is divided into various wards, each of which is represented by a councilor. The administration of Jabalpur is also overseen by various government departments and agencies, including the police department, the public works department, and the health department. The city is also home to various educational institutions, including universities, colleges, and schools, as well as various industries, including textiles, engineering, and pharmaceuticals. In summary, Jabalpur has a rich history and has been an important center of administration for centuries. Today, the city is governed by the Jabalpur Municipal Corporation and is an important center of administration, education, and industry in Madhya Pradesh.

Agriculture

Jabalpur district has a favorable climate for growing oilseed, pulses, cereals, and horticultural crops, with an annual rainfall of 1358 mm. The maximum temperature is in May (40-43°C), and the minimum temperature is in January (8-10°C). The main crops grown in the district are Paddy, Pigeon pea, Soybean, Maize, Sesame in Kharif, and Wheat, Gram, Pea, and Mustard in Rabi. The total area under irrigation is 28%, out of which only 18% is double-cropped. The district has 1393 villages and is divided into 7 blocks. The major soil type is medium and black soil, and the cropping intensity is 136.45. The nutrient consumption (NPK) is 39:21:2. The total population of the district is 2151203, and the number of farmers is 923863. The productivity of important Kharif and Rabi crops of the district varies. The soil type and its characteristics include heavy soil, medium and black soil, and light soil, each with different characteristics. The agricultural scenario in the district is that 80% of the population is dependent on farming, and most of the farming is rainfed. The thrust areas identified based on Participatory Rural Appraisal of the district are Enhancement of Seed Replacement rate, Integrated Nutrient Management, Soil & Water Management, Integrated Pest and Disease Management, Integrated Farming Systems, Improvement in cropping intensity in rainfed areas, and Proper utilization of natural resources in crop production.
